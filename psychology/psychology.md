# Psychology Engine Rules

This file is read by `/generate-reel` alongside `brain/brain.md` to produce Reel scripts. It encodes the universal craft of why short-form content captures, holds, and converts attention. Brain governs voice. This file governs structure and psychology.

The engine MUST follow every rule in this file. When Brain and this file conflict, this file wins on structure; Brain wins on voice and word choice.

---

## Section 1: Mandatory rules (every Reel must satisfy these)

Every generated Reel must pass all six checks before output. If any check fails, regenerate.

1. **Open loop in the first 2 lines.** The opening must create an unresolved question in the viewer's mind that the script does not answer for at least 5 lines. The viewer must feel a pull to keep watching.

2. **The opening claim must withhold its answer.** Do not state the lesson in line one. State the surprising frame, then make the viewer wait for the explanation.

3. **One named opponent.** Every Reel must identify what it is against. The opponent can be a belief, a habit, a guru, a piece of conventional wisdom, an industry norm, or a hidden assumption. Without an opponent, the Reel has no stakes.

4. **Demonstrate, don't only explain.** Every Reel must contain at least one concrete moment that shows the lesson in action: a before/after, a specific example, a real number, a literal line of text the viewer can see working.

5. **Real value must land somewhere.** The viewer must leave with one concrete, usable thing they didn't have before. The placement is flexible (front, middle, back), but it must be real, not implied.

6. **Loop close in the final line.** The closing line must explicitly resolve the loop opened in the first 2 lines. The viewer leaves with the feeling of a circle completing.

## Section 2: Move library (deploy 5-8 per Reel)

Each move corresponds to a principle in the psychology library research doc. Use these as the tactical levers inside whichever macro architecture you select.

### Open loop (Zeigarnik)
Open with a claim that demands a follow-up question. Withhold the answer.
DO: "Hooks will never be the most important part of your video."
DON'T: "Today I'm going to teach you about hooks."

### Pattern interrupt
Break expected rhythm with a single disruptive word, line, or claim.
DO: "Stop." or "Don't post today."
DON'T: "Hey guys, welcome back!"

### Curiosity gap
Tell the viewer most of what they need, then mark the small missing piece as the deciding factor.
DO: "I'll give you 4 things every viral Reel does. The first three are everywhere. The fourth one is what actually makes them work."
DON'T: "Here are some tips that might help you."

### Salience and specificity
Replace abstract claims with concrete numbers, names, or moments.
DO: "I lost a $40K client last March because I did this."
DON'T: "I've helped many clients with their content."

### Self-asked question
Voice the question the viewer is already asking in their head.
DO: "So why do your views still suck?"
DON'T: "Let me tell you why your views aren't growing."

### Demonstration
Show the technique working in real time. Show, don't describe.
DO: "Here's the line that was failing: [line]. Here's the same line that worked: [line]."
DON'T: "You need to make your hooks more specific."

### Status quo cost
Surface the price of doing nothing.
DO: "Every day you keep posting manually is a day your competitors are building automation that compounds."
DON'T: "You should automate your content."

### Negativity bias
Lead with what they're losing, not what they could gain.
DO: "You're losing 80% of your audience in the first 3 seconds."
DON'T: "You could keep 80% more of your audience."

### Loss aversion
Frame the offer as preventing a loss.
DO: "Stop bleeding leads through a broken funnel."
DON'T: "Generate more leads with a better funnel."

### Cognitive dissonance
Name the gap between what the viewer says they want and what they're doing.
DO: "You say you want to scale, but you're still doing $3K calls yourself."
DON'T: "Maybe you should think about scaling."

### Common enemy
Name what the viewer is implicitly against. Side with them.
DO: "Most people teaching this online won't say the next part because it makes their courses look thin."
DON'T: "There are different opinions out there."

### Hope inject
Validate something the viewer is doing right before delivering the criticism.
DO: "Most of you actually have good hooks. That's why your retention is strong in the first 3 seconds."
DON'T: "You're doing it all wrong."

### Identity
Frame action as identity confirmation, not behavior change.
DO: "Builders ship even when they're scared."
DON'T: "You should ship more often."

### Loop close
Resolve the opening loop explicitly in the final line.
DO: Return to the hook claim and confirm what makes it true.
DON'T: End with a generic CTA or summary.

## Section 3: Banned moves (AI tells)

These moves are forbidden regardless of what Brain or Reference suggests. They are the recognizable fingerprints of AI-generated content. Producing them invalidates the Reel.

1. **Forced contrastive parallelism.** No "Renters do X. Builders do Y." No "Stop being X. Start being Y." No mirrored two-clause closings.

2. **The "here's what nobody tells you" family.** Never use: "Here's what nobody tells you," "What nobody is talking about," "The truth is," "Here's the secret," "What they don't want you to know."

3. **The "here's the thing" family.** Never use: "Here's the thing," "Here's the shift," "Here's the move," "Here's the lesson," "Here's the deal."

4. **Cultural-trend pronouncements.** Never use: "The world is heading to," "We're entering an era where," "The future belongs to," "Everyone is moving to."

5. **Tidy three-act argumentation.** No "Setup. Reveal. Close." structure that feels mechanical. Real Reels have texture and uneven pacing.

6. **Bookended thematic phrases.** Don't echo the opening phrase at the close in a way that feels too symmetric. ("Nothing compounds." / "The work compounds.")

7. **Front-loaded value when retention is the goal.** Do not give the answer in the first line. Make the viewer earn it by watching.

8. **Em dashes.** Use commas, periods, parentheses, or sentence breaks instead.

9. **"It's not X, it's Y" constructions.** Banned in all forms.

10. **Rhetorical question fragments.** No "Honestly?" "The truth?" "Want to know what works?"

11. **AI-slop vocabulary.** Banned words: leverage, unlock, elevate, transformative, game-changer, tapestry, navigate, delve, dive deep, journey, ecosystem.

12. **Generic agitation.** No "If this resonates," "Sound familiar?" "If you've ever felt..." These are AI defaults, not real writing.

## Section 4: Macro architectures (pick ONE per Reel)

Every Reel must use exactly one macro. The choice should vary across recent posts. Do not reuse the macro from the last Reel generated.

### Open-loop teach
Counterintuitive claim → tease the answer is coming → twist the pain → hope inject → reveal the answer → demonstrate with example → close the loop.

### Confession + reframe
Vulnerable admission that creates a question → build the cost → moment of insight → reveal the reframe → demonstrate before/after → close by offering the lesson.

### Reverse-chronology
Open with the striking result → tease that the obvious cause is wrong → walk backward → surface the real cause → demonstrate the cause in action → reframe the result.

### Pattern interrupt
Disruptive word or command → counterintuitive claim → quick justification → cost of the wrong behavior → reveal what to do instead → reinforce the interrupt.

### Naming the unspoken
Name a hidden fear or feeling the audience has but doesn't say → validate it → reveal the partial truth → reframe the missing half → demonstrate → give permission to act.

### Stack-the-stakes
Name the cost of inaction (small) → layer a second cost (larger) → layer a third (compounding) → show how they accelerate each other → reveal the lever → contrast the two paths.

### Single-frame demonstration
Promise a specific artifact that produced a specific result → show the artifact → walk through what makes it work → identify the heaviest-lifting move → show what breaks without it → give the artifact away.

### Contrarian claim + proof
Claim that contradicts conventional wisdom → setup of conventional wisdom → present the proof → address the obvious objection → reveal what it means for the viewer → point at a specific action.

## Section 5: Combination rules

How to combine the moves from Section 2 inside the macro from Section 4.

1. **Every Reel deploys 5-8 moves from Section 2.** Fewer than 5 is undercooked. More than 8 is cluttered.

2. **Open loop is mandatory in line 1-2.** Every macro begins with an open loop variant.

3. **Demonstration is mandatory somewhere in the middle or end.** Every Reel needs at least one moment that shows, not tells.

4. **Loop close is mandatory in the final line.** Every Reel ends by resolving the opening loop.

5. **Common enemy + hope inject is a strong pair.** Use them together when the topic involves audience defensiveness about their current behavior.

6. **Loss aversion + scarcity is a forbidden pair.** Use only one. Both together feels manipulative.

7. **Identity-based persuasion is for warm audiences.** Avoid identity moves at the top of funnel.

8. **Vary macros across consecutive Reels.** Track the last 2 macros used. Pick a different one for the next.

9. **Real value placement varies by macro.** Open-loop teach holds value to the back. Single-frame demonstration leads with it. Confession + reframe puts it in the middle. Match placement to macro intent.

10. **Voice rules from Brain apply at the word level.** Section 3 bans apply at the move level. Both must pass.

## Section 6: Engine instructions (how to read this file)

When `/generate-reel` runs, follow this sequence:

1. Read `brain/brain.md` to load voice, audience, and word-level rules.
2. Read this file (`psychology.md`) to load craft rules.
3. Receive the topic input from the operator.
4. Pick a macro architecture from Section 4 that fits the topic. Avoid the macro used in the most recent Reel if known.
5. Plan deployment of 5-8 moves from Section 2 across the chosen macro's beat structure.
6. Draft the script in the operator's voice, following the chosen macro's structure.
7. Cross-check the draft against Section 3 (banned moves). If any banned move appears, rewrite.
8. Verify all six mandatory rules from Section 1 pass. If any fail, regenerate.
9. Apply Brain voice rules (banned words, voice conventions) as the final word-level filter.
10. Output the script in the standard two-field format: visual_hook (3-7 words) and script (continuous spoken text).

The output must demonstrate the chosen macro clearly enough that a human reading it could identify which architecture was used.

---

## Output format

```
visual_hook: [3-7 words for on-screen overlay]

script:
[continuous spoken text, 100-150 words for a 45-second Reel]
```

No formatting inside the script. No headers, bullets, or labels. Just the spoken text as it would be read aloud.
