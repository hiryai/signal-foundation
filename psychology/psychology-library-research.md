# Psychology Library Research

A glossary of established psychological principles that govern why short-form content captures attention, holds it, and converts it into action. This document is research input. It is raw material for the deployable engine file (`psychology.md`). It is not yet structured for engine consumption.

Each entry uses the established academic name as primary, notes the craft term content creators commonly use, defines the principle in plain language, and gives a deployment example showing how the principle appears in real content.

---

## Attention principles
*How content captures attention in the first 1-3 seconds.*

### Zeigarnik effect
**Source:** Bluma Zeigarnik (1927)
**Craft term:** open loop
**Principle:** Unfinished tasks and unresolved information occupy mental real estate more than completed ones. The brain cannot let go of an opened loop until it closes.
**Deployment:** Open with a claim that demands a follow-up question, then withhold the answer. "Hooks will never be the most important part of your video. They'll always be the second most important part." The implicit question (then what is the most important part?) holds attention until the script answers it.

### Pattern interrupt
**Source:** Milton Erickson (clinical hypnosis literature, 1950s); later popularized in NLP and Robert Cialdini's *Pre-Suasion*.
**Craft term:** scroll-stopper, disrupt
**Principle:** When the brain encounters something that breaks an expected pattern, it pauses to evaluate the anomaly. This brief evaluation window is the gap where attention gets captured.
**Deployment:** Open with something visually, tonally, or conceptually unexpected. A whisper in a feed of shouting. A counterintuitive claim where everyone else is agreeing. A sudden silence in a fast-cut video.

### Curiosity gap
**Source:** George Loewenstein (1994), *The Psychology of Curiosity*
**Craft term:** information gap, curiosity bait
**Principle:** Curiosity is triggered by the gap between what we know and what we want to know. The gap must feel close enough to bridge but not so wide it feels hopeless.
**Deployment:** Tell the viewer almost everything they need, then mark the small missing piece as the deciding factor. "I'll walk you through the 4 things every high-converting Reel does. The first three are everywhere on YouTube. The fourth one is what actually makes them work, and almost nobody teaches it." The viewer can sense the missing piece is right there, which is what triggers the urge to keep watching.

### Salience and specificity
**Source:** Chip and Dan Heath, *Made to Stick* (2007); rooted in Tversky & Kahneman's work on representativeness.
**Craft term:** concrete details, show don't tell
**Principle:** Concrete sensory detail is processed faster, remembered longer, and trusted more than abstract description. The brain treats specificity as a credibility marker.
**Deployment:** Replace "I help people grow their business" with "I helped a chiropractor in Ohio go from 3 to 47 patients a week in 90 days." Numbers, places, and named details create trust the abstract version cannot.

## Retention principles
*How content holds attention past the hook.*

### Self-generation effect
**Source:** Slamecka & Graf (1978)
**Craft term:** self-asked question
**Principle:** Information the brain generates itself is remembered and felt more deeply than information passively received. When a viewer arrives at a question on their own, the answer hits harder.
**Deployment:** Set up the implicit question in the viewer's mind, then ask it explicitly. "So what's really keeping you stuck as a small creator?" The viewer was already wondering. Voicing the question lets them feel the script is reading their mind.

### Dual coding theory
**Source:** Allan Paivio (1971)
**Craft term:** demonstration over explanation, show the work
**Principle:** Information processed through both verbal and visual channels is encoded twice and retained more reliably than information processed through one channel alone. Demonstrations create stronger memory traces than explanations.
**Deployment:** Don't explain a technique. Show it working in real time. For example, take a generic line ("Here's my morning routine that changed my life") and on screen, rewrite it live into the version with a curiosity hook ("Here's my morning routine that changed my life, and the one habit my therapist told me to stop doing"). The viewer sees the technique deploy and watches it land.

### Status quo bias
**Source:** Samuelson & Zeckhauser (1988)
**Craft term:** comfort interrupt
**Principle:** Humans default to what they're already doing, even when better alternatives are obvious. Effective content surfaces the cost of the status quo so the brain stops treating it as free.
**Deployment:** Make the cost of inaction visible. "Every day you keep posting manually, even with good prompts, is a day your competitors are building AI agents to do it autonomously." The viewer's current behavior now has a price tag attached.

### Negativity bias
**Source:** Roy Baumeister et al., *Bad Is Stronger Than Good* (2001)
**Craft term:** pain framing, problem-aware open
**Principle:** Negative information weighs more heavily on attention and memory than positive information of equal magnitude. Threat detection is older and faster than reward seeking.
**Deployment:** Lead with the loss, not the gain. "You're losing 80% of your audience in the first 3 seconds" lands harder than "You could keep 80% more of your audience." Same fact, opposite framing, different impact.

## Persuasion principles
*How content shifts belief or behavior.*

### Loss aversion
**Source:** Kahneman & Tversky (1979), *Prospect Theory*
**Craft term:** fear of missing out, downside framing
**Principle:** The pain of losing something is psychologically about twice as strong as the pleasure of gaining the equivalent. People work harder to avoid loss than to achieve gain.
**Deployment:** Frame the offer as preventing loss rather than producing gain. "Stop bleeding leads through a broken funnel" outperforms "Generate more leads with a better funnel" even when the underlying claim is identical.

### Cognitive dissonance
**Source:** Leon Festinger (1957)
**Craft term:** belief-behavior gap
**Principle:** When a person holds two contradictory beliefs, or when their behavior contradicts their stated beliefs, they experience psychological discomfort and seek to resolve it. Content that surfaces a dissonance creates pressure for change.
**Deployment:** Name the gap between what the viewer says they want and what they're actually doing. "You say you want to scale, but you're still doing $3,000 discovery calls yourself." The viewer either changes the belief or changes the behavior.

### Social proof
**Source:** Robert Cialdini, *Influence* (1984)
**Craft term:** testimonials, results, "join the others"
**Principle:** When uncertain, people look to the behavior of others, especially others they identify with, to determine what's correct. Visible adoption signals reduce risk and accelerate decisions.
**Deployment:** Specific numbers and identifiable groups beat generic crowd appeals. "Over 200 coaches in our community use this, and they're coaches like you doing $20K to $50K months." The combination of a specific count plus an identifiable peer group makes the claim feel real rather than marketed.

### Authority
**Source:** Robert Cialdini, *Influence* (1984); also Stanley Milgram's obedience studies (1963)
**Craft term:** credentials, proof
**Principle:** People defer to perceived expertise, especially in domains they don't fully understand. Authority signals (credentials, results, recognition) short-circuit the evaluation process.
**Deployment:** Demonstrate authority through specific outcomes rather than claims. "I helped my clients earn $2M with these strategies" lands harder than "I'm an expert in content strategy."

### Reciprocity
**Source:** Robert Cialdini, *Influence* (1984); originally Marcel Mauss, *The Gift* (1925)
**Craft term:** value-first, free training
**Principle:** When someone gives us something genuinely useful, we feel a near-universal pull to give back. The pull operates whether the value arrives early or late, as long as it actually arrives. The viewer must leave the script having received something real and usable.
**Deployment:** Deliver complete, usable value somewhere in the script (not necessarily at the start). High-performing Reels often hold the real lesson until the back half so retention stays strong all the way through. The "second most important part" Reel structure works because the viewer must watch the entire video to learn the actual answer, but when they do, they leave with a complete technique they can deploy immediately. Reciprocity is satisfied because the value is real, not because it's front-loaded.

### Identity-based persuasion
**Source:** Henri Tajfel, social identity theory (1979); popularized by Seth Godin and James Clear.
**Craft term:** "people like us," tribal framing
**Principle:** People are more persuaded by appeals that align with how they see themselves than by appeals based on logic or self-interest. "I am the kind of person who..." is a stronger lever than "I should..."
**Deployment:** Frame action as identity confirmation rather than behavior change. "Builders ship even when they're scared" outperforms "You should ship more often" because the first invites the viewer to claim an identity rather than perform a behavior.

### Common enemy / out-group framing
**Source:** Henri Tajfel, social identity theory; Cialdini on tribal persuasion.
**Craft term:** us vs them, calling out the gurus
**Principle:** In-group identity is reinforced by defining what the group is against. Naming a shared opponent creates solidarity and motivates action.
**Deployment:** Identify what the viewer is implicitly against (the gurus, the old way, the corporate playbook) and position the content as siding with them against it. "The most engaged creators know this. But they secretly don't want you to know it so you can't catch up to them."

### Reactance
**Source:** Jack Brehm (1966)
**Craft term:** "but you are free," autonomy preserve
**Principle:** When people feel their freedom of choice is threatened, they push back, often by doing the opposite of what's being suggested. Persuasion that preserves autonomy bypasses this resistance.
**Deployment:** End with autonomy intact rather than a hard close. "You don't have to do this. But if you do, here's what changes." The viewer feels chosen, not pushed, and is more likely to comply.

## Conversion principles
*How content moves attention into action.*

### Scarcity
**Source:** Robert Cialdini, *Influence* (1984)
**Craft term:** urgency, deadline, limited spots
**Principle:** Things that appear rare or fleeting are valued more than abundant or persistent ones. Scarcity activates loss aversion at the point of decision.
**Deployment:** Specific scarcity (12 spots, closing Friday) outperforms vague scarcity (limited time only). The brain treats specific constraints as real and vague constraints as marketing.

### Endowment effect
**Source:** Richard Thaler (1980)
**Craft term:** ownership framing, "your" language
**Principle:** People value things they perceive as theirs roughly twice as highly as things they don't yet own. Framing the offer as something the viewer already possesses or has earned increases its perceived value.
**Deployment:** Use possessive language to transfer ownership before the sale. "Your content engine" rather than "a content engine." "The audience you're going to build" rather than "an audience."

### Loop close / payoff
**Source:** Tied back to Zeigarnik effect; closure provides the resolution the open loop demanded.
**Craft term:** stick the landing, payoff line
**Principle:** A script that opens loops without closing them leaves the viewer unsatisfied even if the content was strong. The dopamine of resolution is what makes the script feel complete.
**Deployment:** Return to the opening claim, question, or tension and resolve it explicitly in the close. The closing line should call back to the hook and confirm the lesson has landed, so the viewer leaves with the feeling of a circle completing.

## Composite principle: Hope inject before pain

Some craft moves don't map cleanly to a single academic principle but show up consistently in high-performing content. "Hope inject," the move of giving the viewer a small win before delivering the lesson, is one of these.

**Closest academic anchors:** Self-affirmation theory (Claude Steele, 1988), which shows that affirmation reduces defensive reactions to threatening information; and reactance reduction (Brehm), which shows that preserving autonomy increases compliance.

**Principle:** Viewers brace for pain. A small validation before the lesson disarms the bracing reflex and lets the lesson land. "Most of you are already using good hooks. That's why your retention is strong in the first 3 seconds." The viewer relaxes. Now they're ready to learn what's actually wrong.

**Deployment:** Identify what the viewer is doing right before identifying what they're doing wrong. The asymmetry of expectation (they expected criticism, got partial validation) creates the opening.

## Notes on use

These principles are not independent. A single 30-second Reel may deploy 6 to 10 of them in sequence: open loop in line one, pattern interrupt in line two, common enemy in line three, hope inject in line four, demonstration in lines five through eight, loop close in line nine. The interaction of principles is what creates retention. Any single principle in isolation is not enough.

A critical principle to internalize: real value (the actual technique, lesson, or insight) does not need to appear early in the script. In fact, the highest-retention Reels often hold the real value until the back half, because retention is what the algorithm rewards. The reciprocity principle is satisfied when the viewer leaves with something real, not when the value arrives in line one. Front-loading value can undercut retention. Holding it (while keeping the viewer's curiosity active throughout) is often the more powerful play.
